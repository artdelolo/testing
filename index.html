Save Lake O

Save Lake O is a collaboration between University of Miami students and Florida Audubon to protect the habitats of Lake Okeechobee, wildlife and surrounding communities by communicating the growing dangers of nutrient-rich runoff and the toxic blue-green algae blooms they cause.

Lake Okeechobee

Sometimes referred to as Florida’s inland sea, Lake Okeechobee is the largest freshwater lake in Florida. With an impressive surface area of 730 square miles, the name “Okeechobee” is the Hitchiti word meaning big water. The lake is vital to the surrounding animal and plant life and serves as an essential source of water for people and farms in the region.

Lake O’s Health is at Stake

In recent decades, Lake Okeechobee and surrounding areas have been battling harmful algal blooms caused by excessive nutrients from agricultural and urban activities. The lake is filled with nutrient-rich runoff containing fertilizer, manure, and sewage. They get heated in high temperatures in the summer months that cause the exponential growth of the toxic blue-green algae. The build-up of these algae gets spread by the heavy rains of the wet season, leading them to flow into the Lake and then toward Florida’s west and east coast as water is released through the canal systems.

What Are Hamrful Algal Blooms
& What Causes Them?

“Nutrients” may sound like a positive term, however, it isn’t in the case of a nutrient-rich runoff. Nutrient runoff is a form of water pollution made up of mainly nitrogen and phosphorus coming from lawn and garden fertilizers. Manure, pet and wildlife waste are also sources of nutrients that can get trapped in the soil. When it rains, the nutrients eventually flow into the lake producing colonies of algae. They grow out of control and become “harmful algal blooms” consisting of cyanobacteria. Cyanotoxins are one of the most powerful natural poisons that can cause serious illness and make people, their pets and other animals sick.

Impact on Biodiversity

Many animals including birds, marine mammals, fish, and shellfish are susceptible to the toxins produced by harmful algal blooms. Many animals become ill or die when they consume toxin-contaminated fish or invertebrates. Animals can serve as early indicators of algal bloom toxicity, increases in mortality or morbidity of these animals may be a sign that HAB toxins are present.

The marshes of Lake Okeechobee abound extraordinary species and wildlife. Yet, population decline of wetlands-dependent species have been on a rise due to algal blooms and rapid fluctuations of water levels between wet and dry seasons. Dr. Paul Gray explained,” Lake Okeechobee is a paradise of biodiversity. Wetland communities fill almost one-third of the lake, providing prime habitat for Everglade snail kites, a variety of wading birds, ducks, and game fish and other species. Water levels between 12.5 and 15.5 feet protect these important resources.” During the wet season when water level rises above 16 feet, the submerged marshes begin to die. Deep water affects the wet prairie communities of wading birds to forage. Once plant communities are lost, habitat for fish spawning is lost which causes a disruption in the lake’s food chain. Fast rising water also drowns alligators and birds nests. Population crashes affect many wetlands-dependent species such as Florida apple snails, frogs, aquatic snakes, turtles, round-tailed muskrats. To conserve the environment and the wildlife, proper water level management is crucial to protect and sustain the delicate ecosystem of Lake Okeechobee.

What Can We Do?

Working together to fix this problem.

We are the problem, but we are also the solution. By following some simple steps, we can help prevent algal blooms.

Accurate identification of harmful algal blooms is the first step to protecting your family, pets, and surrounding wildlife from the negative effects of algal toxin exposure. Depending on the waterbody, a potentially harmful bloom may appear in parallel streaks or clumped dots. Some may look like spilled blue, green, white paint or even turn the water to a bright emerald green.

Following yard work, leaves, grass and other debris should be bagged and removed from the property to prevent them from decaying. Animal waste as well, contains nitrogen and bacteria that can pollute the waterways when it rains. When organic materials decompose in freshwater, they release undesirable nutrients that are responsible for producing algae growth. It is important to dispose organic materials properly to reduce excess nutrients from building up and entering the waterbody.

Allow native flowering, deep-rooted vegetation to grow 3 to 5 feet from the edge of the lake or pond shoreline in a vegetative buffer. This method of building a buffer zone would stop runoff containing sediment, trash, and other organic materials from entering lakes and ponds during rainstorms.

For lakes and ponds with chronic nutrient problems, the application of “phosphorous-locking” technologies, such as Phoslock and Alum, can make a significant impact. When applied by a licensed professional, these products can improve water clarity and reduce undesirable nutrients by removing free reactive phosphorus from the water.

Knowing where HABs are occurring—and whether the government is responding—is a critical first step to keeping our waterways, families, and ecosystems safe. Sometimes we do not understand that a solution will take several years of progress. In this time, politicians who started working on possible solutions may not be reelected.

Young people may not know about the cause and effect of the algal bloom issue. Teachers should strive to raise awareness, educate and encourage the younger generation about how to get involved and be a part of the solution.

On farms, regenerative practices that improve soil health help to capture rain where it falls instead of allowing nutrient-packed runoff to wash into bodies of water. We can plant cover crops, diversify what’s grown, reduce tillage, and properly apply compost and manure to prevent water runoff.

Farmers and cattlemen are stewards of the land. We need to take care of endangered species and habitats. We need to have water reservoirs, and make sure everything is done based on science.

Government agencies and us have a common goal. We can work with NGOs and University of Florida to help with research that informs best practices.

We can control the annual contribution of phosphorus by paying attention to the four major fertilization factors (4Rs); right rate, right source, right time and right place. By choosing the right fertilizers with fewer phosphorus, we can help keep soil healthy and prevent algal blooms from forming.

Expand adoption of good farming practices by partnering with states and others to financially reward farmers who plant cover crops. Improve federal initiatives like the federal crop insurance program to empower farmers to adopt regenerative farming practices. Incentivize smart cultivation practices in the Farm Bill, a law that sets agricultural policies.

It’s not just farms that produce tainted runoff. Every year, an estimated 10 trillion gallons of untreated stormwater washes off paved surfaces, through storm sewer systems, and into waterways. This runoff can contain high levels of nutrients that can trigger HABs. Using more green infrastructure in cities can be a solution. Adding green roofs, planting trees, and building gardens of rain-absorbing plants all capture rainwater before it flows into waterways.

Healthy levels of dissolved oxygen in the lake are crucial. Oxygen helps break down the decaying vegetation and other nutrients in the water. Aeration is an environmentally friendly cleaning method that rejuvenates water bodies by increasing the level of oxygen in the water. This technique avoids the use of chemicals and is suitable for use in larger ponds.

Improved monitoring and increased research into harmful algal blooms is much needed to raise awareness of the issue. A nationwide system for collecting data on and responding to HABs can help forecast future blooms and communicate it to the public. These efforts could speed up emergency response and reduce harm to the environment, wildlife, and people.

To preserve conditions of the lake and wildlife, new developments should focus on implementing sewer system connections rather than the use of septic tanks to avoid complications of leakage linked to HABs. With suitable encouragement and incentives from the government, developers can begin to allocate green areas to help nurture the citizen’s relationship with nature at a community level. Having a dedicated group to mobilize initiatives and efforts could attract stakeholders and encourage positive actions in future infrastructure or redevelopments.
